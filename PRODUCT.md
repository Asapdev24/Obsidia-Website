# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Operations managers and founders at 20-100 person SMBs that have outgrown their manual processes and know it, but lack the internal technical resources to fix it. They are not evaluating a platform; they are hiring a firm. They are competent, time-poor, and skeptical of agencies. They have been burned by vague proposals before. They want to understand what they are getting before they commit.

Secondary decision context: the person reading the site may forward it to a partner, a CFO, or a board member. The writing must hold up to that second read.

## Product Purpose

Obsidia builds custom workflow automations, websites, and applications for growing businesses. Three disciplines, one partner. The company's core differentiator is its methodology: audit operations first, identify the highest-value fixes in priority order, build to a signed-off spec, and hand over fully documented so the client's team can run it without Obsidia in the room. Success looks like a client who understands exactly what was built, why, and how to operate it, and who gets measurable ROI within weeks of launch.

## Brand Personality

Sharp. Refined. Resolute.

Voice: declarative, spare, confident without bravado. Writes like a well-structured brief, not a pitch deck. Uses specific language where vague language would be easier. Does not use exclamation marks or startup-friendly encouragement. Earns trust through specificity, not reassurance.

Emotional goal: the reader should feel that Obsidia already understands their problem before they have described it, and that engaging them is the intelligent, low-risk decision.

## Anti-references

- Generic digital agency aesthetics from Clutch or Upwork listings: stock photography, rainbow service icons, "We deliver results" headlines
- SaaS purple-gradient look: Webflow, Framer, Notion — the whole family of "friendly enterprise" with soft purples, rounded everything, and microcopy that congratulates you for clicking a button
- YC startup aesthetic: Inter typeface, rounded buttons, warm cream backgrounds, friendly and approachable — Obsidia is not trying to feel approachable, it is trying to feel precise
- Gulf region competitor sites that look like PowerPoint decks: clip art, mismatched fonts, service grid with icons, no design system
- Template-like output of any kind: anything that looks like it was built from a ThemeForest purchase or a Framer template
- Gradient text: decorative and meaningless
- Big number hero-metric dashboards: the SaaS "72% faster" stat-band formula

## Design Principles

1. **Process as product.** The audit-design-build-handoff methodology is the core differentiator. The site should demonstrate rigor structurally, not just claim it in copy. Section flow, information density, and specificity of language should all reflect a company that thinks before it builds.

2. **Restraint signals competence.** Obsidia's clients are skeptical of oversell. Every visual element must justify its presence. Decoration for its own sake reads as insecurity. Whitespace, sparing use of color, and precise typography carry more credibility than a busy layout.

3. **Speak to the operator.** The user is a functional manager or founder, not a technical buyer and not a casual consumer. The site should feel like a well-written proposal, not a marketing brochure. Tone stays professional and direct; it never congratulates the reader for considering Obsidia.

4. **Legibility is trust.** Typography hierarchy, line length, and copy density should feel like a considered document. If a section reads like a slideshow, rewrite it. Specific outcomes, specific commitments, and specific process steps outperform vague claims at every level.

5. **Earn authority, don't claim it.** No "industry-leading" or "best-in-class". Authority is expressed through the methodology being visible: here is what we do, here is in what order, here is what you receive at each stage. The site should make a skeptical reader think "they clearly know what they are doing" without Obsidia ever saying it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA compliance. No reduced-motion restrictions; animations run for all users. No specific known user needs beyond standard best practices. Ensure sufficient color contrast on all text, keyboard navigability on all interactive elements, and appropriate ARIA labels on icon-only controls.
